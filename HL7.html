HL7 Focus Session

Roundhouse, Lower Campus

4pm-6pm, Saturday 18th November, 2023

The HL7 standards represent a very important and necessary framework for the standardisation of health communication and information exchange on an international scale. The thesis centres around data interoperability, facilitating the secure access of relevant electronic health records when and where it is needed.

HL7 stands for "Health Level 7". The 7 refers to the seventh layer of the ISO made of Open Systems Interconnection model (OSI). The standards were created by a not-for-profit organisation founded in 1987, Health Level 7 International. The purpose is to standardise, and therefore make easier, the exchange of any healthcare related data between the myriad of computer systems underlying the healthcare system and its points of service. The standards aim to do this specifically at the application level of the OSI model, which is an abstraction layer that deals fundamentally with the input and output of data. This is to say that it is concerned with how information about some relevant healthcare endpoint, such as a patient's medication, their diagnosis, or the results of their most recent bloodtest, is organised, exchanged, and ultimately outputted.

In mediating the way in which data is organised and exchanged, HL7 defines a set of messaging standards. When a specialist needs to access patient records to assess their health and form a care pathway, they may need to access information coming from several different computer systems in pathology, radiology, family clinic and so on. Unless all of the computers hosting that data speak the same language, the specialist will be unable to access those records and make informed decisions based on the whole picture. The system is not interoperable.

This could for example lead to a duplicated blood test or imaging procedure, which would cost more time and money for the healthcare provider, and potentially the patient as well.

And so HL7, and the associated Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR), tell software vendors how they should organise data so that it can be sent to and read by other computer systems, therefore allowing users to exchange information efficiently, and in the healthcare context, give staff the whole picture when they need it to make informed decisions about someone's health and wellbeing.

As software and network standards evolve, so too must the messaging standards. Representatives from HL7 international will be talking about how they keep up with the rapid rate of advancement of software and network engineering, and how it can make healthcare systems more holistic in their services to patients, even as the OSI model it was originally based on is superseded.
