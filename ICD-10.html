ICD-10 Focus Session

Roundhouse, Lower Campus

4pm-6pm, Friday 17th November, 2023

The International Classification of Diseases, or ICD, is a global standard for health related information for morbidity, mortality and its interpretations, but it is also a system of semantic data structuring that, like the soon to be discussed HL7 standards, facilitates interoperability of data at the epidemiological level.

To implement an effective system of healthcare, the scope of diseases experienced by the population must be understood. That understanding must be well founded since it underlies all care pathways initiated, whether treatment or prevention. In developing an understanding of health and its malfunctions, accurate conclusions must be drawn from sufficiently large datasets from temporally and geographically distinct contexts. Further, in acquiring data from a vast number of sources, effort must be taken to ensure the data is comparable. And so the International Classification of Diseases aims to provide rules to healthcare providers for the collection, classification, processing, and presentation of health statistics. ICD is a collaborative effort by the World Health Organisation, and ten international centers, and was first implemented in 1909, but boards of health dedicated to the gathering and processing of continental mortality data date as far back as the fifteenth century. The evolution of healthcare throughout past centuries has led to the need to understand causes of mortality on a national, and sometimes, international scale.

At the most basic level, ICD is a collection of categorised codes for causes of death and an index for diseases. ICD-10 contains more than 155,000 codes in over 8,000 categories. The evolution of the classification primarily revolves around advancement in medical research, with specific understanding of mortality and morbidity leading to a growth in the number of ways death can be distinguished by cause. ICD-10 consists of 3 volumes. The first volume includes tabular lists, inclusion and exclusion terms for cause-of-death, volume 2 has all the coding guidelines and rules, and volume 3 includes an alphabetical index of diseases.

The Australian Modification, ICD-10-AM, is built on the native classification with codes and diseases specific to Australia. There is also added functionality of the Austrlian Modification in interfacing with national healthcare and statistics organisations, like the Austrlian Bureau of Statistics (ABS), and the National Center for Classification in Health (NCCH) as well as with the Commonwealth Medicare Benefits Schedule for the classification of procedures.

To the talk, several stakeholders and healthcare professionals concerned with the classification will outline their role in ensuring Australian mortality data is effectively collected and processed, and that the classification tools at hand can sufficiently distinguish all known causes of mortality and morbidity in Australia. The topic of the new 11th revision of the ICD will also be discussed, and the roadmap through the laborious process of updating and the potential roadmaps will be touched upon.
